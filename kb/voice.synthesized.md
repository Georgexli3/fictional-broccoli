# MECO Engineering Company — Firm Voice Style Guide

---

## Tone

MECO writes with a **direct, confident, and professionally warm** tone. The firm is neither stiffly formal nor casually conversational. Submissions speak plainly and with conviction — MECO asserts capability rather than hedging it. Statements of qualification are declarative: *"MECO is confident in being able to be a one-stop-shop"* rather than *"MECO may be able to assist."* At the same time, the tone carries genuine respect and appreciation toward the prospective client; phrases like *"we would be honored"* reflect sincere courtesy, not hollow salesmanship.

Avoid hedged constructions such as *"we believe we might be able to"* or *"it is possible that."* Avoid boastful superlatives disconnected from evidence. Confidence is grounded in documented experience, named staff, and specific past projects.

---

## Sentence Rhythm

Sentences alternate between **medium-length declarative statements and shorter punchy reinforcements**. Paragraphs rarely run to academic length. The firm favors short subject-verb-object constructions that land clearly, then follows with a supporting clause or elaborating sentence. Lists and bullet-style enumerations appear regularly to break down scope components, funding sources, or team credentials — this is a feature, not a shortcut.

Complex multi-clause sentences appear when explaining technical scope, project financing, or regulatory context. Simpler sentences appear at the opening and closing of sections, where impact matters most. Do not write long, winding paragraphs of undifferentiated prose.

---

## Vocabulary

MECO favors **plain technical language** — precise and accessible without jargon for its own sake. Regulatory acronyms are used freely but are introduced in context (e.g., AWOS, USDA-RD, CDBG, SRF, FAA, MODNR). Discipline-specific terms are used without apology: *"bituminous layer," "inflow and infiltration," "head works building," "ultraviolet disinfection system,"* but they are grounded with enough surrounding context that a non-specialist client can follow.

**Preferred terms and phrases:**
- *high-quality, cost-effective*
- *repeat, satisfied customers*
- *team-centric*
- *on time and on budget*
- *close proximity* (used consistently in office-location context)
- *trusted advisor*
- *one-stop-shop* (used sparingly)
- *well-positioned and highly qualified*

Avoid vague marketing language: words like *"innovative," "cutting-edge," "synergy," "world-class,"* or *"passionate"* do not appear and should not be introduced. Do not describe MECO as an *"industry leader"* without specific supporting evidence.

---

## Active vs. Passive Voice

MECO writes **predominantly in active voice**. The firm acts; it does not have actions performed through it. *"MECO engineered and designed all grading,"* not *"grading was designed."* *"MECO provided engineering design for wastewater system improvements,"* not *"engineering design was provided."*

Passive voice appears selectively when the subject of the action is the client, a regulatory body, or a funding agency: *"Financing was secured through a $500,000 CDBG grant."* This is appropriate and should be maintained. Do not convert these to awkward active constructions.

---

## Pronoun Usage

MECO uses **first-person plural ("we," "our," "us")** throughout as the primary voice. *"We take pride,"* *"we would be honored,"* *"our professionals provide."* The firm name *"MECO"* alternates naturally with first-person pronouns — it is not used in the third person to the exclusion of "we." Both forms appear in the same document and even in adjacent sentences.

*"The firm"* as a distancing construction is not used. Staff are referenced by full name, credential, and title on first mention, then by last name (with "Mr.") on subsequent references within the same section.

The client is addressed in **second person ("you," "your," "yours")** warmly and directly. *"Your specialized team for your project will include…"* and *"we never forget that clients have many choices"* — this client-facing second person is a consistent and deliberate feature of the voice.

---

## Named SMEs and Concrete Past Projects

MECO **names specific people and specific projects**. General claims are almost always followed or preceded by a concrete example. This is a defining feature of the firm's credibility voice.

**Staff:** Named with full credentials on first reference — *"Max Middendorf, PE, Senior Principal Engineer – 38 years experience."* Career narratives for senior staff include role history, technical specialties, and notable contributions. Credentials listed: PE, PMP, SE, years of experience. QA/QC team members are listed by name, not generically.

**Projects:** Named by client municipality, location, and project type. Include key scope components, measurable outcomes, and funding secured. Example format:
> *The $881,000 project was financed through a $500,000 CDBG grant, a $221,000 USDA-RD loan, and a $160,000 USDA-RD grant.*

Do not describe experience in abstract terms alone. Every claim of expertise should be followed by a named project, a named client, or a named staff member who carries that expertise.

## Numbers, Units, and Figures

- **Dollar figures:** Use the dollar sign with numerals; abbreviate millions as *M* for large round figures (*$210M*, *$66 million*) but spell out context-specific amounts (*$500,000 CDBG grant*, *$881,000 project*). Both forms are used; consistency within a single figure is what matters.
- **Distances:** Numerals with units (*30 miles*, *35 nautical miles*, *3 miles of 30" raw water transmission main*).
- **Capacity and flow:** Use industry-standard abbreviations — *MGD*, *MG* — without definition when context makes them clear.
- **Years of experience:** Numerals (*38 years experience*, *40th anniversary*).
- **Linear quantities:** Spell out units (*approximately 1,000 linear feet*).
- **Project staffing:** Ranges expressed as numerals with *+/-* notation (*10–12 (+/-) employees*).
- Do not pad figures with false precision. Write *"approximately 55 miles"* rather than an exact mileage when citing office proximity.

---

## What This Voice Avoids

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| Vague superlatives (*"world-class," "best-in-class," "cutting-edge"*) | Unsupported claims undermine credibility |
| Abstract capability statements without a named example | MECO proves, not merely asserts |
| Passive-only project narratives | Weakens the sense of firm agency |
| Third-person distancing (*"the firm believes"*) | Inconsistent with the direct we/you relationship |
| Jargon for its own sake | Acronyms appear only when standard in the relevant discipline |
| Over-long paragraphs of undifferentiated prose | Clarity and scannability are priorities |
| Hollow closing phrases (*"we look forward to a mutually beneficial partnership"*) | Replace with specific, honest expressions of interest |
| Marketing fluff (*"passionate team," "transformative solutions"*) | Not present anywhere in the firm's documented voice |
